Premature Rupture of Membranes

Posted on Jan 20, 2021

Motivating Example

A 26-year-old G2P0101 woman who is 12 weeks pregnant, presents to your prenatal
clinic for a new patient visit. Her history is significant vaginal delivery 12
months ago. Beginning at 24 weeks in her previous pregnancy, the patient
presented numerous times to Labor and Delivery reporting contractions, and
she was sent home each time with a diagnosis of “Braxton-Hicks contractions.”
She eventually presented at 28-weeks gestation and was diagnosed with preterm
labor. She delivered at 29 weeks. The neonate’s course was complicated by
intra-ventricular hemorrhage and respiratory distress syndrome. The child
now appears to have cerebral palsy and chronic lung disease due to
bronchopulmonary dysplasia.

0. What is definition of preterm labor?

  • Between 20 weeks and 37 weeks

1. What are the risk factors for preterm labor? Which risk factors does she have?

  • #1 risk factor = previous preterm pregnancy; patient has this
  • polyhydramnios
  • short cervical length; LEEP or Cone bx from abnormal pap smear
  • placental abruption (from 2nd trimester bleeding); note: smoking disrupts the intima in vessels
  • infection (chorioamnionitis)
  • had amniocentesis
  • genital tract infection
  • connective tissue disorders (Ehlers Danlos)
  • short inter-pregnancy interval (<6-18 mos)

2. What characteristics distinguish Braxton-Hicks contractions from true labor contractions?

  • labor contractions: 45+ seconds; every 2-3 mins
  • Braxton hicks; CTX do NOT have increasing intensity and increasing frequency

3. What should you counsel the patient regarding the signs and symptoms of preterm labor?

  • Note: obstetric visit history questions: 1. fetal movement, 2. contractions, 3. leakage of fluid, 4. bleeding

4. What recommendations, if any, would you discuss with this patient regarding prevention strategies to reduce the risk of preterm delivery in this pregnancy? To reduce the risk of neurodevelopmental disorders and other morbidity associated with preterm labor in this fetus should she experience preterm labor?

  • reduce the risk of preterm delivery: tocolytics
  • antenatal corticosteroids: betamethasone, dexamethasone: promotes fetal lung maturity in the absence of intra-amniotic infection before 32-36 wks GA
  • folic acid (1st trimester)

5. If the patient does experience PTL in this pregnancy, what recommendations would you make regarding treatment and management? When would you consider tocolysis (i.e. giving medications in an attempt to stop contractions)?

progesterone

tocolysis: indomethacin, nifedipine/terbutaline (CCB), mag sulfate; stop this after 48 hrs; do not give at all, after 34 wks – at this point, the risk of preterm labor regarding the fetus are at lowest level at 34 wks, so don’t give tocolytics after 34 wks

if term: first check GBS status and fetal presentation; then labor may be induced, or pt can be observed for 6 hours

if 34-36 GA: labor induction may be considered

if <32 weeks GA: expectant management with bed rest and pelvic rest

if signs of infection or fetal distress, give ABX (ampicillin/penicillin/gentamicin) and induce Labor

GBS prophylaxis: penicillin

6. What are potential adverse outcomes of preterm birth for the fetus?

  • risk to baby (for delivering preterm)
    • Brain: intraventricular hemorrhage (b/c blood vessels are not supported well) => seizure
    • Bowel: necrotizing enterocolitis, sepsis
      • baby doesn;t have as good resistance to infxn, so bugs enter bowel
    • Lung: resp distress => bronchopulmonary dysplasia
  • born before 27 wks vs after 27 wks
    • before AND after: all above, + developmental abnormalities
Your patient has progressed well in her pregnancy. She is currently receiving
17-OH-progesterone weekly and she overall feels well. She calls the triage line
to report some vaginal leaking and is unsure if her “water is broken.” She
denies any pain or bleeding. She is currently 35-weeks gestational age.

7. What risk factors are associated with premature rupture of membranes (PROM)?

  • previous PPROM
  • genital tract infection
  • antepartum bleeding
  • smoking: associated with PPROM, as well as IUGR, abruption

8. How do you evaluate a patient with concern for PROM?

  • diagnosis
    • gush of amniotic fluid on speculum exam
    • ferning on microscopy of vaginal fluid
    • amnisure
    • false positives on nitrazine test: lube, sexually active, semen, bleeding

9. How would you counsel her about delivery?

  • complications of PROM
    • MC: infection (chorioamnionitis)
    • smaller uterine size
      • placental abruption/rupture
      • cord can come out of cervix / get compressed because you’re not having as much buffer along uterine walls

10. How would you manage this patient after confirmation of PPROM?

  • if 34+ wks = DELIVER
  • steroids
  • if have infxn = delivery immediately
  • if no infxn = monitor them and wait until 34 wks to induce
  • if infxn (chorioamnionitis)
    • amoxicillin/ampicillin
    • AND erythromycin
    • 2 days IV and 5 days oral

other

[IMPORTANT] if water broke BEFORE 20 wks vs after 20 wks, what is difference in outcome

BEFORE 20 wks: inadequate lung development; baby may not be able to breathe (even if you intubate, give surfactants, etc)

  • baby may not survive because of respiratory insufficiency

when to deliver for pt with shorter cervical length and PROM:

  • MC complication is infection
  • delivery within 72 hrs; 95% of ppl devliery within 1-2 hrs
  • obgyn
