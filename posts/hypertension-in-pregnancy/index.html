Hypertension in Pregnancy

Posted on Jan 20, 2021

Encounter

A 22-year-old G1P0 currently at 38 0/7 weeks presents for her routine prenatal
visit. She has had an uncomplicated pregnancy up to this point, with the
exception of a late onset of prenatal care and obesity (BMI of 35 kg/m2).
She reports that during the past week, she has noted some swelling of her
hands and feet. She also has been feeling a bit more fatigued and has had a
headache on and off. She reports good fetal movement.

She has had some contractions on and off, but nothing persistent. Her blood
pressure is 147/92 and her urine dip has 1+ protein/no ketones/no glucose.
The fundal height measures 36 cm, the fetus is cephalic with a heart rate
of 144 bpm. On physical exam you note that the patient has 3+ pre-tibial
edema, and trace edema of her hands and face. She has 2+ deep tendon
reflexes and 2 beats of clonus. You review her blood pressures up to this
point and note that at the time of her first prenatal visit at 18 weeks,
her blood pressure was 130/76 and she had no protein in her urine. However,
since that visit, her blood pressures seem to have been climbing higher
with each visit. Her last visit was one week ago, and she had a blood
pressure of 138/88 with trace protein in the urine and she has
gained 5 pounds.

1. What is considered a hypertensive blood pressure during pregnancy?

  • BP threshold for dx of HTN in pregnancy = 140/90 and new onset proteineuria;
    • 2 BP taken between 4 hrs apart => diagnosis of gHTN

2. What types of hypertensive syndromes can occur during pregnancy?

Chronic Hypertension: SBP >= 140 or DBP >= 90. Pre-pregnancy, prior to < 20 weeks GA, > 12 weeks postpartum

Gestational Hypertension: SBP >= 140 or DBP >= 90 (>4 hours apart), 20 weeks GA, No proteinuria or systemic signs/symptoms

Preeclampsia: SBP >= 140 or DBP >= 90 AND New onset proteinuria (P:C >= 0.3 mg/dL, or protein >= 300mg/dL 24 hrs) OR Systemic signs or symptoms

Chronic Hypertension and Superimposed Preeclampsia: Chronic hypertension as defined above, >= 20 weeks GA, Worsening hypertension, New onset proteinuria

3. What are the risk factors for preeclampsia?

  1. first pregnancy AND obesity
  2. Maternal risk factors: dz-related (CHTN, CRD, SLE, DM, AA), immune-related (nulliparity, multiple gestation, new father), family hx, age, high BMI
    • note: preE more common in FIRST pregnancy
    • age: AMA and very young

4. How does the physiology of preeclampsia lead to the clinical symptoms and findings?

  • pathogenesis
    • endothelial damage => edema
    • blood clots (like DIC)

5. What are the history, physical exam, and laboratory findings that support a diagnosis of preeclampsia-eclampsia syndrome?

  1. physical exam

    • pulmonary edema (crackles)
    • seizure = eclampsia

  2. labs: CBC, LFTs, Cr

    • low platelets <100K
    • elevated AST, ALT
    • Cr > vs baseline (normal would be .4 to .8)

  3. Severe Preeclampsia: BP > 160/110 w/ proteinuria > 5 g/24 hr (or > 3-4 dipstick); associated w/ certain conditions (HA, AMS, vision, RUQ pain (from swollen liver), liver fxn, oliguria, pulm edema, thrombocytopenia)

    • protein cutoffs: P/C > 0.3 or >300 over 24 hrs
    • visual change: dark spots in vision (scotoma)

6. What types of maternal and fetal complications are associated with preeclampsia-eclampsia syndrome?

end organ damage

- brain bleed;
- cerebral edema => herniation
- liver damaage (swells and rupturee)
- cardiac dysfunction

complication of pregnancy

- growth restriction
- placental abruption
- on US: oligohydramnios (because of placental insufficiency)
- FHR: late decels, decreased HRV

7. What are some interventions that can be done at outset of pregnancy to reduce risk?

  • on admission, put on left side, and recheck BP after 20 mins

8. What are common antihypertensive therapies for women with pre-existing hypertension?

  • antihypertensives
    • DO NOT use: ACE-i (teratogen)
    • CAN use: labetaolol and hydralazine

9. What additional monitoring is performed for women with pre-existing hypertension?

  • things to do to decrease risk of preE
  • US every 4 weeks
  • NST’s to monitor baby
    • weight reduction
    • ASX
    • delivery at 38-39 wks if cHTN; NEVER deliver beyond EDD
    • delivery at 37 wks if preE
    • delivery immediately, even if early (e.g. 28 wks) if SEVERE preE (HELLP, liver damage, headache, vision change) or Eclampsia

10. When is delivery usually recommended?

Normally: 37 weeks - induction;

severe preE before 34 wks

- Prevent eclampsia (MgSO4) - seizure prophylaxis
- Control maternal BP (hydralazine, labetalol)
- Deliver fetus (betamethasone)
- methyldopa
- check the baby- HTN can cause FGR from placental insufficeincy

if preE, low platelets, need C section, use GENERAL anesthesia and give plts

- do not give spinal anesthesia because even minor bleeding can paralyze the pt

if mild preE at 34 wks:

- defeintely deliver them at 37 wks
- see them 1-2x weekly (monitor more frequently: BPP, NST)
- check labs more frequently

11. In patient diagnosed with preeclampsia with severe features, what additional steps are taken in pregnancy to help prevent development of eclamptic seizures?

Magnesium sulfate

19. What are long-term health implications of hypertension in pregnancy?

Future risk of preeclampsia

other

if pt starts bleeding, uterine pain, and has pre-E

- c/o abruption
- need to do VAGINAL delivery (risk of bleeding is more with C section)
- uterus stop bleeding by increasing tone => blood vessels get closed (when it contracts, the muscle fibers are "clamping the blood vessels")
  • when patients have twins - higher risk of preE because MORE PLACENTAL tissue
  • obgyn
