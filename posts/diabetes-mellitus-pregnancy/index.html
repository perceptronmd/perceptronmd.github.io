Diabetes Mellitus in Pregnancy

Posted on Jan 20, 2021

Encounter 1

37yo Hispanic woman G2P1001 at 10w3d comes today for her new OB visit. She is
overall doing well but has concerns from her previous pregnancy. Her first
pregnancy was complicated by a diagnosis of gestational diabetes mellitus.
She worries about this diagnosis for this pregnancy.

She wonders how common is GDM and why she had it in her previous pregnancy. How do you counsel her and what are your next steps?

DM risk increases in preg because of decreased insulin resistance

screen at 24-28 wks

Note: the insulin need NORMALLY increases further in pregnancy;

if pt has decreased need for insulin, worry about growth restriction

How do you interpret the results?

  • glucose test
    • 1 -hour test: 50g load
    • 2-hour test: only do this postpartum
    • 3-hour: do if they fail the 1-hr; 100g load; need 2 values abnormal

Who should be screened early?

  • previous pregnancy complicated by GDM
  • known glucose intolerance
  • obesity [link]

“One study performed in India recommended early universal screening at the first prenatal visit for all women due to the 11-fold increased risk of developing GDM when compared with Caucasian women. This study also showed increased association of GDM with increasing age, parity, family predisposition, and infertility [8]. Additionally, women who are diagnosed with GDM should be screened 6-12 weeks postpartum with the 75g oral glucose tolerance test due to the increased rate of progression to type 2 diabetes in these women [2].

[8] Neelakandan R, Sethu PS. Early universal screening for gestational diabetes mellitus. J Clin Diagn Res. 2014;8(4):OC12-4. DOI: http://dx.doi.org/10.7860/JCDR/2014/8199.4264. [2] Committee on Practice Bulletins—Obstetrics. Practice Bulletin No. 137: Gestational diabetes mellitus. Obstet Gynecol. 2013;122(2 Pt 1):406-16. DOI: http://dx.doi.org/10.1097/01.AOG.0000433006.09219.f1."

Encounter 1, continued

In your astute history taking, you find out that she never had a 2h GTT following her pregnancy.
You perform an early glucose tolerance test and diagnosis her with pregestational diabetes. She is interested in understanding the impact that the diagnosis for this current pregnancy for her and her growing fetus.

The patient starts a diet and exercise program and monitors her blood sugars, fasting and 2 hours postprandial. However, as her pregnancy progresses, she finds that it is more challenging to maintain normal blood sugars despite diet and exercise.

How do you counsel about treatment? What are the benefits?

  • monitoring for baby
    • serial growth US
    • NST after 32 wks
    • delivery early (if really uncontrolled, delivery at 34-36 wks)
    • have mother check sugars 4x/day

Your patient successfully delivers a healthy baby girl. She comes to the office for her postpartum visit and asks what she can do to better understand her risk for diabetes in the future. What are your next steps?

  1. screen for overt diabetes: check glucose concentrations for 24 to 72 hours after delivery to exclude ongoing hyperglycemia
  2. screen for depression: PPD more common in patients with diabetes vs others
  3. breastfeeding: improves maternal glucose metabolism and thus may reduce the glucose levels obtained during a postpartum glucose tolerance test (GTT). Several prospective studies have reported that breastfeeding decreased the long-term incidence of type 2 diabetes after a diagnosis of GDM compared with not breastfeeding.

Encounter 2

23yo G1 at 6w3d comes for her new OB visit. She has a history of
insulin-dependent Type 1 DM. She is curious what her prenatal care will be like.

What are the risks for her?

  • gestationl hypertension
  • preeclampsia
  • long term: type 2 diabetes, obesity, impaired glucose tolerance

What are the risks for her growing fetus?

- c section
- shoulder dystocia
- polyhydramnios - increased chance preterm lablor, PROM, **obstructed bowel**
    - poly leads to breech presentation (because theres more space in uterus)
    - umbilical cord prolapse - can happen in poly because of unengaged head
    - when uterus deflates suddenly (decrease in size) - placental will separate (palcenta won't just shrink with the uterus)
- placenta will NOT increase with size of baby; baby is fixed as to how much oxygen it can extract; sugars will make baby acidotic => tissue damage (birth abnormalities if before 12 wks)
    - if insult baby BEFORE 12 wks
        - insult can be anything: infexn, high sugar, chemicals
        - abnormal baby => **birth defects**
    - if you insult the baby AFTER 12 wks = **growth abnormalities**, fetal death
    - hint: if you give sugar water w/o oxygen -- you start fermenting, make alcohol

- abnormalities: skeletal, CNS, heart
    - sacaral agenesis
    - caudal regression syndrome
    - spina bifida
    - andecelpha
    - transposition of great arteries
- HbA1c vs birth abnormalities
    - <6.5: same risk as any othe rperson
    - >10: cutoff for HIGH risk (20%) of birth abnormality

Other notes

White classification

* A1 GDM: requires insulin
* A2 GDM: does NOT require insulin

other things to check in GDM

- HAVE: risk of preE - check CMP
- DO NOT have: ketoacidois, diabetic retinopathy, kidney disease (dont have enough time to cause that damage)

when pts have kidney disease, are smoker, etc => placental insufficiency

- if pt is smoker AND has GDM => causes premature lungs in baby
- lungs dont mature well - baby can be full term and still have respiratory distres syndrome
